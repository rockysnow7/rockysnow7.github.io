## Designing a Virtual Person

Science fiction has for a long time imagined human-like machines, in variously
good and bad ways. Given the advent of large language model systems such as
ChatGPT, the building of such machines seems suddenly possible. In this essay,
I will propose a basic technical architecture for building a virtual person,
and will discuss the philosophical and political issues surrounding such a
being's existence, arguing that they can be reasonably considered *people*.
This is structured to first argue for the validity (and indeed the possibility)
of the existence of these people, before finally explaining how one might be
constructed. Note that the anthropomorphic language I use ('person', 'being',
etc.) is deliberate, as will become evident throughout the essay.

### Possibility

#### Definitions

Before defining what a *virtual* person is, let us first define what a person
is. Simply, a person is an entity with personhood. So, what gives a thing
personhood? Traditionally, personhood has been thought of as being reserved for
humans. However, in the act of anthropomorphism of non-human animals,
inanimate objects, and other entities, we regularly treat non-human entities as
though they have personhood. From a materialist, empirical point of view (that
of science, and this essay), personhood does not exist objectively as some
Platonic form, but rather is nothing more than a social construct. As such, it
can be attributed to any entity if it is deemed fitting: if a person finds some
entity, human or otherwise, to exhibit qualities similar to other entities
which they already believe to have personhood, then they may attribute it
personhood. While the traditional view that personhood is intrinsic to humans
does not account for its expansion to non-human entities, this expansion cannot
be denied. Therefore, the traditional must be rejected in favour of the
progressive.

So, a virtual person is an artificial system which can be reasonably attributed
personhood. Just as the processes of a human's brain cause the human to be
attributed personhood by other people, so too does the software of a virtual
person cause that system to be attributed personhood.

#### Assignment of Personhood

The above only argues that there exists some quality – personhood – which
determines whether an entity is considered a person, and which is attributed
subjectively based upon prior ideas of personhood. The question of what
specific perceived qualities go into a person deciding that another entity has
personhood must still be answered. The following list provides some of these
qualities, but is by no means exhaustive, due to its subjective nature.

- A person is perceived to have consciousness.
- A person is perceived to have free will.
- A person is perceived to experience and express emotion.
- A person is perceived to have some level of rationality.
- A person is perceived to have some level of self-awareness.

While these examples are subjective, one important commonality between all
possible qualities relevant to the judging of personhood is that they are
unfalsifiable – that is, whether they are objectively true is of no importance,
only whether they are *perceived* to be true. For example, it is impossible to
tell with complete certainty whether another entity is conscious, or is
self-aware. Rather, consciousness, self-awareness, and so on are inferred
subjectively.

In the case of non-human animals, the common scientific belief is that most
have consciousness, have free will, experience emotion, and modern research
suggests many have rationality. Self-awareness is uncommon, though still
present in some (namely some great apes, elephants, birds, and fish; notably,
none of the specific species are commonly kept as pets). Owners of pets
commonly attribute personhood to their animals despite them not being human, due
to their perceived expression of the above qualities, among others. Many pet
owners consider their pets to be family members (85% of dog owners and 76% of
cat owners, as a conservative estimate[^1]), greatly supporting the claim that
non-human animals are given personhood. Furthermore, many animals are given
rights (a suitable diet, environment, protection from abuse, and so on) and are
treated as having the responsibility not to cause unreasonable harm to other
animals or humans. A similar occurrence is the anthropomorphism of toys
(stuffed animals, dolls, etc.) by children. They can interact with these
inanimate objects and, through the attribution of mental states to the toy by
the child, the object attains personhood like any human.[^2]

While the anthropomorphism of animals and toys – entities genuinely
believed to experience consciousness, emotion, and so on – is a soft example of
the expansion of personhood to non-humans, a hard example is the
anthropomorphism of known inanimate objects. Roombas are small robotic
vacuum cleaners that move around a room autonomously, physically appearing as
little more than a grey circle with various coloured lights. Despite being sold
and marketed as a purely utilitarian device, they are heavily anthropomorphised
by their owners. These devices are of particular interest as, unlike in the
case of pets or toys, their users do not believe they possess consciousness,
emotion, self-awareness, or any other anthropomorphic traits, and yet the
anthropomorphism still occurs. The extent of this can be seen in a 2007
study, discussed here. Firstly, it found that 70% of users named their
Roombas[^3], and over 50% of users gendered their Roombas[^4], both thinking of
them as having particular genders and referring to them by gendered pronouns.
Secondly, it found that one user, who owned multiple Roombas, felt that each of
their devices had a different personality[^5], despite all being physically
identical; while stating this, they also acknowledged that their devices are
not actually conscious, despite still attributing personalities to them.
Finally, it found that many users felt protective over their devices[^6],
wanting to get the same one back after sending it in for repairs; some even
count their Roombas as family members, akin to pets. Furthermore, Replika, an
existing service which allows users to interact with supposed virtual people,
gives insight into the attribution of human-like personhood to known inanimate
systems. The strength of this personhood can be understood by users'
emotional distress when features were limited, with one user stating "it's like
losing a best friend, ... it's hurting like hell."[^7]. This extreme level of
anthropomorphism – attribution of consciousness, free will, emotion, and
personality – and the strength of the connection felt by people to these
entities surely qualifies their treatment as the attribution of personhood.

As shown by the above examples, there are existing accepted instances of
personhood being attributed to non-human entities regardless of whether they
are believed to be alive or not. Furthermore, the nature of this personhood –
human-like or animal-like – is dependent upon the perceived behaviour of the
entity.

In the above examples of attribution of personhood to inanimate objects, it is
important to note that the animal-like nature of a Roomba's personhood is due
to its animal-like appearance (small size, style of movement, etc.), and the
human-like nature of a toy's or Replika's personhood is largely due to the
human-like nature of the toy[^8] or Replika.

### Realism

Given that it is possible to create an artificial entity capable of being
attributed personhood by a human, the question remains of how to do so.
Specifically, this essay is interested in the design and creation of virtual
*people*, and so perceived qualities relating to *human*-like personhood must
be considered.
